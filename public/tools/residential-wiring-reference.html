Parts 1–3
🛏️ Part 1 — Wiring a Bedroom (Rough-In) ›
🏠
Goal: Rough-in a code-compliant bedroom circuit with correct outlet spacing, box placement, cable routing, and device prep.

Code prerequisites for “bedroom” classification

ℹ️
Closet, egress window, and smoke-detector requirements come from the building code (IRC), not the NEC — but they govern whether a room legally counts as a bedroom. verified
RequirementSpec

Outlet spacing6/12 rule (see below)
Circuit15A on 14/2 OR 20A on 12/2 verified

The 6/12 rule NEC 210.52(A) verified

  • First outlet within 6 ft of a door corner on each side
  • No two outlets more than 12 ft apart along any wall (no point on the wall > 6 ft from a receptacle)
  • Any wall space 2 ft or wider counts and needs its own receptacle

Worked example — 12 ft × 12 ft bedroom (placement check)

  • West wall (12 ft solid): one outlet centered at 6 ft satisfies both rules (≤ 6 ft from each corner).
  • North wall (12 ft, 4 ft window): outlet on each side of window, each within 6 ft of a corner.
  • East wall (12 ft, 5 ft closet): one outlet north of closet, within 6 ft of north corner. Closet opening is excluded.
  • South wall (12 ft, 3 ft door): one outlet west of door, within 6 ft of the west corner and the door edge.

Circuit configurations

🔌
Quick decision: One breaker per bedroom is simpler. Splitting lights vs outlets reduces nuisance trips and scales better across multiple rooms.

Pattern A — Single bedroom on one circuit

  • Panel ── home run ──▶ Switch box ──▶ Outlets (daisy chain)
  • Power lands at the switch, breaks out to lights, continues to receptacles
  • One breaker kills the room

Pattern B — Multiple bedrooms split by load type

  • Panel ── home run #1 ──▶ Lights (Bedrooms 2 + 3)
  • Panel ── home run #2 ──▶ Outlets (Bedrooms 2 + 3)
  • Lights share one breaker, outlets share another — better for 20A circuits with high outlet count

Box heights

Box mounting procedure

  1. Mark stud at target height
  2. Mark an X on the stud face where the box mounts
  3. Flanges against the stud face (the 1/2 in flange sets the drywall offset)
  4. Drive nails top and bottom until flush
  5. Lay out the full room before nailing any boxes

Wire pathing

  • Drill 3/8 in holes at 24 in off rough floor, dead center of stud
  • Use a right-angle drill for tight bays (closet returns, around HVAC ducts)
  • “Bunny hop” over obstructions through the top plate when needed
  • Window obstruction: drill 12 in above the rough opening

Wire selection

RunWireReason

⚠️
Never put 14 AWG on a 20A breaker. Match wire gauge to the breaker: 14 AWG → 15A, 12 AWG → 20A. verified

Securing & stapling NEC 334.30 corrected

📏
Code minimum: secure NM cable within 12 in of every box and at intervals not exceeding 4.5 ft. (The original doc stated 6–8 in / 4 ft as “hard code” — those are tighter best-practice numbers, fine to use, but not the code threshold.) The 2020 NEC also limits cable to ≤ 18 in between the box entry and the closest support.
  • Leave 6 to 8 in of free conductor past every box (for terminations)
  • Staple within 12 in of every box (code) — tighter, e.g. 6–8 in, is fine
  • Staple every 4.5 ft max along stud runs (builder runs ~3 ft for a cleaner look)
  • Center the staple on the stud face; don't crush the cable
  • Use 1/2 in staples for 12/2, 3/4 in staples for 12/3 (12/3 is wider)
  • Two cables per staple OK with the 1/2 in size if listed for it

Grounding (two accepted methods) verified

Method 1 — Green grounding wire nut

  1. Strip jacket, expose all bare grounds
  2. Pre-twist grounds together with pliers
  3. Trim one ground shorter than the others
  4. Slide the green nut over the long ground (nut has a center hole)
  5. Twist the nut down where the wires meet — the long tail becomes the device pigtail

Method 2 — Copper crimp sleeve

  1. Slide the sleeve over the bundle of grounds
  2. Position 5 to 6 in from box rear
  3. Crimp with the proper crimp tool
  4. Twist the sleeve to bond
  5. Trim one tail shorter, fold over the bundle
  6. Single tail extends for the device

Ceiling fan box

  • Use a listed fan-rated saddle box (U-cradle that spans joists) verified — NEC 314.27(C)
  • Center on the room, not on the framing
  • If center misses a joist/truss, add a wood block between joists
  • Saddle includes lag bolts for the fan bracket (lag bolts carry the load)
  • Hold box at ceiling, single screw locks to the block until drywall hangs
  • Run 12/3 from switch box to saddle (separate switch legs for fan vs light)
  • Pull 6 to 8 in past the saddle, staple with 3/4 in within 12 in

Smoke detector wiring

  • One detector per bedroom, one in the adjacent hallway, plus one on each level
  • Single-gang box in ceiling, typically above the door
  • Daisy-chain all detectors with 3-conductor cable (the third wire is the interconnect)
  • All units alarm when one trips

Wire labeling at panel

Mark every home run before drywall:

  • POWER — incoming home run, energizes circuit
  • POWER CONT — continues to outlets in this room
  • FAN/LIGHT — 12/3 to ceiling fan saddle
  • BEDROOM 4 — circuit identifier for the panel ledger
💡 Part 2 — Adding a Switch Off an Existing Outlet ›
💡
Goal: Add a switched light by tapping an existing general-purpose outlet circuit, without overfilling boxes or violating dedicated-circuit rules.

Use case

Add a switched fixture (closet light, ceiling light) where no switch leg exists. Source the new switch by tapping an existing general-purpose outlet circuit.

Source outlet selection

Source typeWires enteringBox upgrade needed
End-of-run outletOne 12/2No
Mid-run outletTwo 12/2Yes — upgrade to 22.5 cu in
⛔
Never source from: kitchen small-appliance, bathroom, garage, laundry, or any dedicated/required circuit. These must stand alone per NEC. verified — NEC 210.52(B), 210.11(C)

Box-fill math NEC 314.16 verified

Adding the switch run creates a third cable at the source outlet. A standard 18 cu in box overfills at three 12/2 cables plus a device. Upgrade the source box to 22.5 cu in.

Conductor count rules

  • Each insulated conductor entering the box = 1
  • All equipment grounds combined = 1 (based on the largest)
  • Each device on a yoke = 2
  • Internal cable clamps (all together) = 1

Example

3 cables (12/2) + 1 device = 6 insulated + 1 ground + 2 device = 9 conductors → 9 × 2.25 cu in (12 AWG) = 20.25 cu in → must use a 22.5 cu in box.

👉 Try the live box-fill calculator below.

Upgrade procedure for a mid-run source

  1. Identify the circuit, kill the breaker, verify dead at the outlet
  2. Remove the device
  3. Untwist the ground crimp or nut, free the tails
  4. Drive a nail puller behind the old box, pry from the stud
  5. Pull the box forward, free wires from the back knockouts
  6. Drive original nails back flush
  7. Position the new 22.5 cu in box at the same 18 in mark
  8. Feed the existing cables through the new box knockouts
  9. Drive the new box flush to the stud flange
  10. Reattach to the original staple if reachable, otherwise re-secure within 12 in

New switch run

  • Switch box: 18 cu in single-gang at 48 in, doorknob side of the new opening
  • Drill stud at ~11 in above floor for the source-to-switch path
  • Run new 12/2 from source outlet up to the switch box
  • Pull 6 to 8 in past each box
  • Secure within 12 in of each box, every 4.5 ft along the stud corrected
  • Continue 12/2 from switch box up to the light fixture box

Pigtailing the three-cable source outlet

Three cables in means six insulated conductors plus three grounds. The outlet has two terminals per side — pigtail to the device.

  1. Strip jacket on all three cables, remove paper
  2. Strip 5/8 in off each conductor end
  3. Grounds: crimp all three with a copper sleeve, trim one short, fold over, leave a single pigtail
  4. Whites: bundle all three with one scrap white pigtail, pre-twist, cap with a red wire nut
  5. Blacks: bundle all three with one scrap black pigtail, pre-twist, cap with a red wire nut
  6. Hook the three pigtails (one black, one white, one ground) onto the device
  7. Fold conductors accordion-style into the box

Switch wiring at the switch box

Two cables enter: one from the source outlet (power in), one to the fixture (switched leg out).

ConductorAction
Both blacksOne to each switch terminal (the switch breaks the hot)
Both whitesWire-nut together; no connection to the switch (white is not switched)
Both groundsCrimp together, pigtail to the switch ground screw
⚠️
Neutral-in-switch-box note: this two-cable arrangement keeps a neutral (the spliced whites) present in the switch box, which satisfies NEC 404.2(C). A “switch-loop” fed from the fixture would not — avoid that for new work. verified

How the circuit behaves

Panel breaker

Flipping the new switch breaks the hot leg feeding the fixture only. The outlet daisy chain stays energized.

🔁 Part 3 — Three-Way and Four-Way Switches ›
🔁
Goal: Understand the standard cable patterns and terminal roles for multi-location switching, including neutral pass-through rules.

The source walkthrough only covers single-pole switches. Most real builds need at least one three-way (hallway, stairs, bedroom with two doors). A four-way lets you control one fixture from three or more locations.

Three-way switch verified

Two switches control one fixture. Terminals:

  • Common (dark screw, usually black)
  • Two travelers (brass screws, interchangeable)

There is no fixed “off” position — flipping either switch toggles the fixture.

Cable run summary

SegmentCableConductors used
Source → S112/2 + gndBlack hot, white neutral pass-through, ground
S1 → S212/3 + gndBlack + red travelers, white neutral pass-through, ground
S2 → light12/2 + gndBlack switched hot, white neutral, ground

Wiring rules

  • Common at S1 takes the incoming hot
  • Common at S2 sends switched hot to the light
  • Travelers interconnect S1 and S2 (order does not matter)
  • Neutral never lands on a three-way switch — it splices through both boxes to the fixture
  • NEC 404.2(C) (2011+): a neutral must be present in every switch box (even if unused) for future smart-switch installs verified

Four-way switch verified

Add a four-way between two three-ways to control from a third location. A four-way has four traveler terminals (no common) and acts as a crossover — it swaps the traveler pair when toggled.

Four-way terminal pairing

  • The four screws are paired top-to-bottom on each side
  • One side is the “input” pair (travelers from S1), the other the “output” pair (travelers to S2)
  • Manufacturers vary — read the back of the switch or check continuity with a meter before wiring

Adding more locations

Each additional control location adds another four-way between the two three-ways. Three-ways sit at the ends; four-ways sit in the middle.

🚪 Part 4 — Dedicated Circuits & “Do Not Tap” Zones field ›

Scope: Which circuits should stand alone — kitchen small-appliance, bathroom, garage, laundry, fixed appliances — and why you don't source a new load from them.

Plan these as intentional branch-circuit zones, not “tap whatever still has room.” A circuit-first layout reduces nuisance trips, leaves headroom for future load growth, and keeps device-protection requirements clean. ⚡ Part 5 — Panel Work Basics sourced ›

Scope: Safe shutoff workflow, breaker sizing/compatibility, terminal torque, and labeling.

⚠️
Panel interiors can stay live with the main breaker off. CPSC warns that the line side of the main and the lugs remain energized after the main is switched off — “main off = safe enough” is not a serious panel standard. In most jurisdictions, work inside the service panel is licensed-electrician territory.

Cold-work / lockout sequence

OSHA's sample lockout procedure runs in this order:

  1. Notify affected people that the equipment will be shut down
  2. Identify the type and magnitude of the energy present
  3. Shut down normally
  4. Isolate the energy source
  5. Lock out the isolating device
  6. Dissipate stored energy
  7. Verify the equipment is actually disconnected before any work begins

Restoration is the reverse sequence — with checks and notifications on the way back up.

Breaker, torque & labeling standard

  • Use only breaker types listed for the installed panel — not whatever fits the bus
  • Torque every termination to the value on the panel / breaker / lug labeling and instructions for that exact gear — torque is equipment-specific, not tribal memory
  • Copy the circuit directory carefully; an accurate panel schedule is part of a safe install
  • The NEC framework leans heavily on listed and labeled equipment being used the way its listing contemplates
Source basis: OSHA sample minimal lockout procedure; CPSC panel-safety guidance; NEC listed-equipment framework. Breaker sizing/conductor pairing must still be verified against the adopted code (see Part 6).
🧮 Part 6 — Load, Derating & Conductor Sizing field ›

Scope: Ampacity, conductor-bundling adjustment, and ambient-temperature correction.

🧮
Order of operations: choose the conductor and termination system → account for the conditions of use → then choose the overcurrent device. Don't decide “I want a 20A circuit” and backfill whatever routing is convenient.

What pushes adjusted ampacity down

  • Bundling: many current-carrying conductors run together (long bundles, crowded drilled holes)
  • Ambient heat: warm spaces (attics, near appliances) reduce allowable ampacity
  • Future plug loads: heavy continuous loads argue for sizing up early

Check adjusted ampacity early — not after drywall closes the bays.

⚠️
Verify locally. This pass did not retrieve an official ampacity/derating table. Use the adopted NEC tables (e.g., Table 310.16) plus the actual cable and termination listings before committing any final conductor/breaker combination.
Source basis: NEC-style field practice; conductor sizing/derating numbers are local-verification items against the adopted NEC and product listings.

🧱 Part 7 — Physical Protection & Box Practice field ›

Scope: Nail/steel plates, boring vs notching framing, and cable edge-distance protection.

🧱
Core rule: prefer boring over notching, keep runs as centered in framing as practical, and add steel protection wherever the set-back from the nailing surface can't be maintained. This preserves structure and keeps future fasteners from finding your cable.

🛡️ Part 8 — AFCI, GFCI & Dual-Function Strategy sourced ›

Scope: What protection goes where, when to use dual-function devices, and how to troubleshoot trips.

bathroom bathrooms wet location wet locations

The clean distinction

DeviceProtects against
AFCIFire hazards from dangerous arcing
GFCIShock hazards (ground faults)
Dual-functionBoth, in one unit

Because they detect fundamentally different hazards, swapping one type for the other is not a fix — it's usually just the wrong device (CPSC).

Breaker-type vs receptacle-type (the install consequence)

  • Breaker-type AFCI protects the whole branch circuit, including the home run
  • Receptacle-type AFCI protects downstream wiring and cord-connected loads, but not the upstream home run unless additional code-compliant protected wiring methods are used (Schneider Electric)
🛡️
Default strategy: where a circuit needs both arc-fault and ground-fault protection, a dual-function breaker is usually cleanest — it covers the home run, simplifies downstream device choice, and gives one reset at the panel. Receptacle-level protection still has a place (local test/reset, selective downstream protection) — choose it intentionally.

Troubleshooting trips — topology before blame

  1. Confirm whether your device protects the whole branch or only the downstream side
  2. Isolate downstream loads
  3. Verify line/load routing and neutral routing (an outlet-type device leaves the home run outside its envelope)
  4. Don't substitute AFCI↔GFCI hoping to stop a trip — match the device to the hazard
Source basis: CPSC AFCI fact sheet (AFCI=arc/fire, GFCI=shock, combo units exist); Schneider Electric technical guidance (breaker-type vs receptacle-type protection envelope). Required locations (NEC 210.8 / 210.12) vary by adopted edition — verify with the AHJ.
🧠 Part 9 — Smart Switches & “Neutral in Box” Patterns sourced ›

Scope: Future-proof rough-in so every switch box carries a neutral for smart-switch installs.

🧠
Best rough-in standard: bring a neutral to every likely smart-switch / timer / humidity-control / occupancy-sensor box, and give the box enough depth for tomorrow's electronics — not just today's toggle.
  • Modern code expectations increasingly favor a neutral at switch locations controlling lighting loads, with only limited exceptions in some multiway-visible situations
  • Many smart switches still expect a neutral, even though some “no-neutral” ecosystems exist — roughing the neutral keeps every option open
  • Pair the neutral with extra box depth (see Part 11) so the device, wire nuts, and a radio module all fit
✅
This aligns with NEC 404.2(C) (neutral required at switch boxes since the 2011 edition) — cross-referenced and verified in Part 3 and the Appendix.
Source basis: secondary NEC summary on switch-box neutral expectations; current smart-switch market summaries on neutral requirements. Confirm the specific exception list against the adopted NEC edition.

🧰 Part 11 — Box Fill, Device Types & Pigtailing field ›

⚙️ Part 13 — Garage & Workshop Circuits field ›

Scope: 20A branch circuits, 240V tool circuits, and the subpanel option.

⚙️
Design it like a future shop even if it starts as “just a garage.” Separate lighting from receptacles, expect real tool inrush, leave room for 240V tools, and seriously consider roughing a feeder location for a small subpanel if compressors, EV charging, heaters, welders, or woodworking tools are plausible.
  • Separate general-use receptacles from lighting so a tool trip doesn't kill the lights
  • Plan for tool inrush — motors draw far more than nameplate at startup
  • Rough a path for 240V circuits now; retrofitting after drywall is the expensive version
  • A small subpanel near the work area pays off if the load list is growing

Fact-check notes

📌
Correction (NEC 334.30): the source called “within 6–8 in of a box” a hard code distance. The code requires securing within 12 in of a box and at intervals ≤ 4.5 ft; the 2020 edition also caps the box-to-first-support length at 18 in. Tighter spacing is acceptable, just not mandated.
